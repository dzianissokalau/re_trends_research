# Real Estate Price Dynamics Research Library

## Purpose
- This file is a working library of high-trust theories, empirical studies, and institutional analyses on residential real estate prices and rents.
- The emphasis is on resources that help explain price dynamics and underlying factors: structure, location, schools, crime, income, labor markets, credit conditions, supply constraints, regulation, amenities, and macro cycles.
- The list is intentionally selective rather than exhaustive. Priority goes to seminal papers, top-field journals, and strong institutional syntheses from bodies such as the IMF and OECD.
- The literature is US- and UK-heavy because that is where the most cited empirical housing work is concentrated.

## Quick Start
- If you want the core conceptual frame for how housing markets work, start with DiPasquale and Wheaton (1992), Rosen (1974), and Poterba (1984).
- If you want to explain prices with property and neighborhood characteristics, start with Rosen (1974), Hill (2011), Black (1999), Gibbons (2004), and Gibbons and Machin (2005).
- If you want to judge whether buy prices look high relative to rents and fundamentals, start with Himmelberg, Mayer, and Sinai (2005), Gallin (2008), Davis, Lehnert, and Martin (2008), and Case and Shiller (2003).
- If you want to explain why some places become persistently expensive, start with Saiz (2010), Hilber and Vermeulen (2016), Gyourko, Mayer, and Sinai (2013), and Hsieh and Moretti (2019).
- If you want to study credit-driven booms and busts, start with Mian and Sufi (2009), Mian and Sufi (2011), Hirata et al. (2013), and Geng (2018).
- If you want to build or validate price indices from sales or listing data, start with Bailey, Muth, and Nourse (1963), Case and Shiller (1987), and Hill (2011).
- If you want rental-market and regulation evidence, start with Gallin (2008), Davis, Lehnert, and Martin (2008), and Diamond, McQuade, and Qian (2019).

## Suggested Reading Order
1. DiPasquale and Wheaton (1992) for the market structure.
2. Rosen (1974) for pricing property attributes.
3. Poterba (1984) for user cost and asset pricing logic.
4. Himmelberg, Mayer, and Sinai (2005) for buy-versus-rent valuation.
5. Saiz (2010) and Hilber and Vermeulen (2016) for supply constraints.
6. Mian and Sufi (2009; 2011) for the credit channel.
7. Black (1999), Gibbons (2004), and Gibbons and Machin (2005) for local amenity capitalization.
8. Hill (2011) and Case and Shiller (1987) for measurement.

## 1. Foundational Theory And Market Structure

### Sherwin Rosen (1974), *Hedonic Prices and Implicit Markets: Product Differentiation in Pure Competition*
- Type: Seminal theory; Journal of Political Economy.
- Summary: The foundational hedonic-pricing paper. It treats observed housing prices as bundles of implicit prices for characteristics such as size, rooms, neighborhood quality, schools, crime, or accessibility.
- Best use when: You want a defensible theoretical base for feature-level pricing models or amenity capitalization studies.
- Main factors: Structural attributes, neighborhood quality, amenities, disamenities.
- Why it matters for your project: This is the main theoretical justification for merging listing or transaction prices with school, crime, labor, transport, and area-quality data.
- Links: [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1086/260169), [EconPapers](https://econpapers.repec.org/RePEc:ucp:jpolec:v:82:y:1974:i:1:p:34-55)

### Jennifer Roback (1982), *Wages, Rents, and the Quality of Life*
- Type: Seminal spatial-equilibrium theory; Journal of Political Economy.
- Summary: Shows how local wages and rents jointly capitalize amenities and productivity differences across places. Important for understanding why high-price areas can also have high wages.
- Best use when: You want to connect housing prices with labor markets, income, local productivity, or place-based amenity value.
- Main factors: Wages, rents, amenities, local productivity, quality of life.
- Why it matters for your project: Useful when combining housing outcomes with labor, earnings, or urban-quality variables and when interpreting cross-area price differences.
- Links: [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1086/261120), [EconPapers](https://econpapers.repec.org/RePEc:ucp:jpolec:v:90:y:1982:i:6:p:1257-78)

### James M. Poterba (1984), *Tax Subsidies to Owner-Occupied Housing: An Asset-Market Approach*
- Type: Seminal housing-asset pricing theory; Quarterly Journal of Economics.
- Summary: A classic user-cost framework for owner-occupied housing. It links house values to interest rates, taxes, expected appreciation, depreciation, and maintenance.
- Best use when: You need a theory of buy prices as asset prices rather than just shelter prices.
- Main factors: Interest rates, taxes, expected appreciation, maintenance, depreciation.
- Why it matters for your project: This is the backbone for buy-versus-rent logic and for research linking mortgage conditions and rates to prices.
- Links: [DOI](https://doi.org/10.2307/1883123), [Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/99/4/729/1896452)

### Denise DiPasquale and William C. Wheaton (1992), *The Markets for Real Estate Assets and Space: A Conceptual Framework*
- Type: Core conceptual framework; Real Estate Economics.
- Summary: Introduces the well-known four-quadrant model linking space markets, asset markets, construction, and stock adjustment. It explains how rents, prices, new supply, and existing stock interact.
- Best use when: You want a market-wide framework for how rents, prices, supply, and stock evolve together over time.
- Main factors: Rents, asset prices, construction, housing stock, adjustment dynamics.
- Why it matters for your project: Particularly helpful because you are collecting both price data and real-estate stock or supply-side information.
- Links: [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/1540-6229.00579), [Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/1540-6229.00579)

### N. Gregory Mankiw and David N. Weil (1989), *The Baby Boom, the Baby Bust, and the Housing Market*
- Type: Demographic housing-demand theory and evidence; Regional Science and Urban Economics.
- Summary: A classic paper linking demographic structure to housing demand and prices. It is often cited in research on cohort effects and long-run housing demand.
- Best use when: You want to test whether age structure, household formation, or population composition is a driver of local prices or rents.
- Main factors: Demography, cohort size, household formation, demand.
- Why it matters for your project: Good theoretical support if you later integrate population and household-composition data.
- Links: [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/0166-0462(89)90048-6), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/0166046289900486)

## 2. Measurement And Index Construction

### Martin Bailey, Richard Muth, and Hugh Nourse (1963), *A Regression Method for Real Estate Price Index Construction*
- Type: Seminal measurement paper; Journal of the American Statistical Association.
- Summary: The classic repeat-sales framework. It uses repeated transactions of the same property to estimate constant-quality price appreciation.
- Best use when: You want to build a robust sales-price index from historical transaction data.
- Main factors: Price index construction, repeat sales, quality adjustment.
- Why it matters for your project: Essential if you plan to convert raw sale records into a research-grade house price index.
- Watch-out: Repeat-sales methods can underweight properties that transact rarely and can miss quality changes between sales.
- Links: [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/01621459.1963.10480679), [Taylor and Francis](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/01621459.1963.10480679)

### Karl E. Case and Robert J. Shiller (1987), *Prices of Single-Family Homes Since 1970: New Indexes for Four Cities*
- Type: Classic empirical methodology; NBER / New England Economic Review.
- Summary: Extends repeat-sales measurement and anchors the logic behind what later became the Case-Shiller style index.
- Best use when: You want a practical bridge between the original repeat-sales idea and modern house-price index construction.
- Main factors: House price index construction, repeat sales, metro-level tracking.
- Why it matters for your project: Useful if you want a benchmark methodology for city or submarket price tracking.
- Watch-out: Like other repeat-sales approaches, it is less informative about the price contribution of changing features.
- Links: [NBER Working Paper](https://www.nber.org/papers/w2393), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.3386/w2393)

### Robert J. Hill (2011), *Hedonic Price Indexes for Housing*
- Type: Methodological survey; OECD Statistics Working Paper.
- Summary: A practical overview of hedonic index methods, including why and how they differ from repeat-sales approaches.
- Best use when: You need to choose between hedonic, repeat-sales, or hybrid index methods for listings and sales data.
- Main factors: Quality adjustment, index design, feature-based modeling.
- Why it matters for your project: Very useful for deciding how to price-adjust data when the housing mix shifts across time or geography.
- Links: [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1787/5kghzxpt6g6f-en), [OECD](https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/economics/hedonic-price-indexes-for-housing_5kghzxpt6g6f-en)

## 3. Buy Versus Rent, Valuation, And Bubbles

### Charles Himmelberg, Christopher Mayer, and Todd Sinai (2005), *Assessing High House Prices: Bubbles, Fundamentals, and Misperceptions*
- Type: High-trust synthesis; Journal of Economic Perspectives.
- Summary: A widely used framework for evaluating whether high house prices reflect bubble-like conditions or fundamentals such as low interest rates, taxes, and rents via user cost.
- Best use when: You want a disciplined way to judge whether prices look stretched relative to rents and financing conditions.
- Main factors: Rents, interest rates, taxes, expected appreciation, user cost.
- Why it matters for your project: Probably the single best short paper to anchor buy-versus-rent valuation work.
- Links: [AEA](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/089533005775196769), [NBER Working Paper](https://www.nber.org/papers/w11643)

### Joshua Gallin (2008), *The Long-Run Relationship between House Prices and Rents*
- Type: Empirical valuation paper; Real Estate Economics / Federal Reserve.
- Summary: Examines whether house prices and rents move together in the long run, which is central to testing housing valuation against fundamentals.
- Best use when: You want a research base for using price-to-rent ratios or co-integration logic.
- Main factors: House prices, rents, long-run valuation, mean reversion.
- Why it matters for your project: Strong support for any workflow that compares transaction or listing prices with rental fundamentals.
- Links: [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/pubs/feds/2004/200450/200450pap.pdf), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1540-6229.2008.00225.x)

### Morris A. Davis, Andreas Lehnert, and Robert F. Martin (2008), *The Rent-Price Ratio for the Aggregate Stock of Owner-Occupied Housing*
- Type: Empirical valuation paper; Review of Income and Wealth.
- Summary: Builds evidence on aggregate rent-price ratios and shows why owner-occupied housing valuation should still be tied to rental fundamentals.
- Best use when: You want to study valuation at aggregate or regional level and need a clean bridge between owner-occupied and rental markets.
- Main factors: Rent-price ratio, valuation, owner-occupied housing.
- Why it matters for your project: Good companion to Gallin and Himmelberg et al. if you want a buy-versus-rent section in later research.
- Links: [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1475-4991.2008.00274.x), [IDEAS/RePEc](https://ideas.repec.org/a/bla/revinw/v54y2008i2p279-284.html)

### Karl E. Case and Robert J. Shiller (2003), *Is There a Bubble in the Housing Market?*
- Type: Influential empirical analysis; Brookings Papers on Economic Activity.
- Summary: A classic behavioral and survey-informed discussion of bubble dynamics, expectations, and price acceleration in housing markets.
- Best use when: You want a historically important frame for expectations-driven housing booms.
- Main factors: Expectations, price momentum, speculative dynamics.
- Why it matters for your project: Helpful when you want to distinguish fundamental drivers from extrapolative beliefs in rapid appreciation periods.
- Links: [Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/is-there-a-bubble-in-the-housing-market/), [JSTOR stable page](https://www.jstor.org/stable/120914)

### Edward L. Glaeser and Charles G. Nathanson (2015), *Housing Bubbles*
- Type: Modern synthesis chapter; NBER / Handbook chapter.
- Summary: A concise overview of theory and evidence on housing bubbles, including credit frictions, behavioral channels, and limits to arbitrage.
- Best use when: You want one relatively compact review before going deep into bubble research.
- Main factors: Credit, speculation, expectations, supply frictions.
- Why it matters for your project: Good orientation resource before using more specialized credit or behavioral papers.
- Links: [NBER Working Paper](https://www.nber.org/papers/w20426), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/B978-0-444-59531-7.00011-9)

### David Genesove and Christopher Mayer (2001), *Loss Aversion and Seller Behavior: Evidence from the Housing Market*
- Type: Seminal behavioral housing paper; Quarterly Journal of Economics.
- Summary: Shows that sellers anchor on past purchase prices and resist losses, which helps explain price stickiness, slow adjustment, and listing behavior.
- Best use when: You want a behavioral explanation for sticky asking prices, slow downward adjustment, or unusual time-on-market patterns.
- Main factors: Seller behavior, reference dependence, price rigidity.
- Why it matters for your project: Particularly relevant for listing-price research rather than only transaction-price research.
- Links: [NBER Working Paper](https://www.nber.org/papers/w8143), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1162/003355301753265561)

## 4. Supply Constraints, Stock, And Spatial Divergence

### Albert Saiz (2010), *The Geographic Determinants of Housing Supply*
- Type: Top empirical paper; Quarterly Journal of Economics.
- Summary: A foundational study showing how geography constrains housing supply elasticity across metros.
- Best use when: You want to explain why similar demand shocks produce very different price effects across locations.
- Main factors: Supply elasticity, geography, land scarcity, regulatory interaction.
- Why it matters for your project: One of the most important papers if you are collecting housing stock and development constraints alongside price data.
- Links: [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1162/qjec.2010.125.3.1253), [MIT Press](https://direct.mit.edu/qjec/article/125/3/1253/18810/The-Geographic-Determinants-of-Housing-Supply)

### Edward L. Glaeser, Joseph Gyourko, and Albert Saiz (2008), *Housing Supply and Housing Bubbles*
- Type: High-impact empirical paper; Journal of Urban Economics / NBER.
- Summary: Argues that supply inelasticity is central to understanding why bubbles and large price swings are more likely in constrained places.
- Best use when: You want to combine bubble logic with supply-side market structure.
- Main factors: Supply elasticity, boom-bust amplitude, local constraints.
- Why it matters for your project: A strong bridge between macro housing cycles and local development constraints.
- Links: [NBER Working Paper](https://www.nber.org/papers/w14193), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jue.2008.07.007)

### Christian A. L. Hilber and Wouter Vermeulen (2016), *The Impact of Supply Constraints on House Prices in England*
- Type: Core UK empirical paper; The Economic Journal.
- Summary: A major study showing that supply constraints are a key reason English house prices respond so strongly to demand growth.
- Best use when: You need UK-specific evidence rather than US metro evidence.
- Main factors: Planning constraints, land supply, local earnings, house prices.
- Why it matters for your project: This is one of the best anchors for England-focused research on affordability and persistent regional price pressure.
- Links: [LSE open version](https://eprints.lse.ac.uk/65993/), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/ecoj.12213)

### Joseph Gyourko, Christopher Mayer, and Todd Sinai (2013), *Superstar Cities*
- Type: High-trust empirical paper; American Economic Journal: Economic Policy.
- Summary: Explains how highly productive and highly desirable cities can become persistently expensive when supply is constrained and demand is concentrated among high-income households.
- Best use when: You want a framework for persistent price premia in top-tier cities rather than short-run cycles.
- Main factors: Income sorting, local demand concentration, supply constraints, urban hierarchy.
- Why it matters for your project: Very useful for interpreting expensive submarkets, especially where labor-market strength and scarcity interact.
- Links: [AEA](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pol.5.4.167), [NBER Working Paper](https://www.nber.org/papers/w12355)

### Chang-Tai Hsieh and Enrico Moretti (2019), *Housing Constraints and Spatial Misallocation*
- Type: High-impact macro-spatial paper; American Economic Journal: Macroeconomics.
- Summary: Shows that housing supply constraints in productive cities can lower national output by preventing workers from moving to high-productivity places.
- Best use when: You want to connect local housing costs to labor allocation, productivity, and macroeconomic efficiency.
- Main factors: Housing supply, labor mobility, wages, productivity, spatial allocation.
- Why it matters for your project: Excellent support if your research later links house prices to labor-market frictions and growth.
- Links: [AEA](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/mac.20170388), [NBER Working Paper](https://www.nber.org/papers/w21154)

## 5. Credit, Interest Rates, And Macro Drivers

### Atif Mian and Amir Sufi (2009), *The Consequences of Mortgage Credit Expansion: Evidence from the U.S. Mortgage Default Crisis*
- Type: Major empirical credit paper; Quarterly Journal of Economics.
- Summary: Shows how mortgage credit expansion contributed to the US housing boom and bust, especially in areas with weaker borrower balance sheets.
- Best use when: You want an evidence base for the credit-supply channel in house-price cycles.
- Main factors: Mortgage credit, leverage, borrower composition, defaults, boom-bust dynamics.
- Why it matters for your project: Strong justification for integrating mortgage, credit, or affordability measures into price-dynamics work.
- Links: [NBER Working Paper](https://www.nber.org/papers/w13936), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1162/qjec.2009.124.4.1449)

### Atif Mian and Amir Sufi (2011), *House Prices, Home Equity-Based Borrowing, and the U.S. Household Leverage Crisis*
- Type: Major empirical macro-finance paper; American Economic Review.
- Summary: Demonstrates how rising house prices relaxed borrowing constraints and amplified household leverage through home-equity borrowing.
- Best use when: You want to study feedback loops between price appreciation, household balance sheets, and spending.
- Main factors: House prices, leverage, borrowing constraints, consumption.
- Why it matters for your project: Useful if you later connect price growth to credit extraction, refinancing, or household demand.
- Links: [AEA](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.101.5.2132), [NBER Working Paper](https://www.nber.org/papers/w15283)

### Huidrom Hirata, M. Ayhan Kose, Christopher Otrok, and Marco E. Terrones (2013), *Global House Price Fluctuations: Synchronization and Determinants*
- Type: IMF cross-country analysis.
- Summary: Studies how global house-price cycles co-move and what macro factors help explain them across countries.
- Best use when: You want a macro benchmark beyond one country or metro system.
- Main factors: Interest rates, credit, income, synchronization, global cycles.
- Why it matters for your project: Helpful for framing local results inside wider international housing-cycle evidence.
- Links: [IMF](https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/WP/Issues/2016/12/31/Global-House-Price-Fluctuations-Synchronization-and-Determinants-40472)

### Nan Geng (2018), *Fundamental Drivers of House Prices in Advanced Economies*
- Type: IMF synthesis and empirical analysis.
- Summary: A practical summary of macro fundamentals behind house prices in advanced economies, including income, rates, demographics, and credit.
- Best use when: You want a concise institutional overview before digging into country-specific work.
- Main factors: Income, demographics, interest rates, credit, supply.
- Why it matters for your project: Good orientation piece for turning a large set of candidate drivers into a manageable research agenda.
- Links: [IMF](https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/WP/Issues/2018/04/06/Fundamental-Drivers-of-House-Prices-in-Advanced-Economies-45725)

### Aida Caldera Sanchez and Asa Johansson (2011), *The Price Responsiveness of Housing Supply in OECD Countries*
- Type: OECD comparative policy paper.
- Summary: Estimates how responsive housing supply is across OECD countries and relates that to regulation and market structure.
- Best use when: You want an internationally comparable supply-side benchmark.
- Main factors: Supply elasticity, regulation, planning, market structure.
- Why it matters for your project: Good institutional complement to Saiz and Hilber-Vermeulen when you want broader comparative context.
- Links: [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1787/5kgk9qhrnn33-en), [OECD](https://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/economics/the-price-responsiveness-of-housing-supply-in-oecd-countries_5kgk9qhrnn33-en)

## 6. Schools, Crime, Transport, And Neighborhood Effects

### Sandra E. Black (1999), *Do Better Schools Matter? Parental Valuation of Elementary Education*
- Type: Seminal local-amenity capitalization paper; Quarterly Journal of Economics.
- Summary: A landmark paper showing that school quality is capitalized into house prices.
- Best use when: You want strong causal motivation for linking school performance data to local price levels.
- Main factors: School quality, neighborhood sorting, house prices.
- Why it matters for your project: One of the best foundational papers if school data is part of your feature set.
- Links: [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1162/003355399556070), [Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/114/2/577/1848115)

### Stephen Gibbons (2004), *The Costs of Urban Property Crime*
- Type: Strong UK empirical paper; The Economic Journal.
- Summary: Estimates how crime is capitalized into property values, giving a direct price interpretation of neighborhood safety.
- Best use when: You want to justify incorporating crime rates or crime categories into house-price models.
- Main factors: Crime, neighborhood quality, local externalities, house prices.
- Why it matters for your project: Highly relevant because you are collecting crime data and need evidence that it matters economically.
- Links: [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.0013-0133.2004.00181.x), [Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/ej/article-abstract/114/499/F441/5089877)

### Stephen Gibbons and Stephen Machin (2005), *Valuing Rail Access Using Transport Innovations*
- Type: Strong UK accessibility paper; Journal of Urban Economics.
- Summary: Shows how improved rail accessibility capitalizes into property values.
- Best use when: You want evidence that transport connectivity and commuting access belong in price models.
- Main factors: Accessibility, transport, commute costs, local prices.
- Why it matters for your project: Useful if you later add station-distance, travel-time, or network-access variables.
- Links: [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jue.2004.10.002), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0094119005000067)

## 7. Rental Markets And Regulation

### Rebecca Diamond, Tim McQuade, and Franklin Qian (2019), *The Effects of Rent Control Expansion on Tenants, Landlords, and Inequality: Evidence from San Francisco*
- Type: Top empirical policy paper; American Economic Review.
- Summary: A major paper on rent regulation showing mixed effects: benefits for incumbent tenants, but also supply and composition responses that can reduce long-run rental availability.
- Best use when: You want serious evidence on rental-market regulation, landlord responses, and distributional effects.
- Main factors: Rent control, rental supply, tenant welfare, landlord behavior.
- Why it matters for your project: Important if you later study rent dynamics, tenancy regulation, or investor responses in rental markets.
- Links: [AEA](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.20181289), [NBER Working Paper](https://www.nber.org/papers/w24181)

## 8. Practical Notes On How To Use These Resources

### If your main question is "What determines house prices at the micro level?"
- Start with Rosen (1974).
- Then read Black (1999), Gibbons (2004), and Gibbons and Machin (2005).
- Use Hill (2011) if you need a feature-adjusted index rather than only a predictive model.

### If your main question is "Are prices high because of fundamentals or because they are stretched?"
- Start with Poterba (1984) and Himmelberg, Mayer, and Sinai (2005).
- Then read Gallin (2008), Davis, Lehnert, and Martin (2008), and Case and Shiller (2003).
- Add Glaeser and Nathanson (2015) if you want a compact bubble-literature map.

### If your main question is "Why are some cities or regions so persistently expensive?"
- Start with DiPasquale and Wheaton (1992), Saiz (2010), and Hilber and Vermeulen (2016).
- Then read Gyourko, Mayer, and Sinai (2013) and Hsieh and Moretti (2019).
- Add Caldera Sanchez and Johansson (2011) for international supply comparisons.

### If your main question is "How do credit conditions amplify housing cycles?"
- Start with Poterba (1984) for the asset-pricing logic.
- Then read Mian and Sufi (2009) and Mian and Sufi (2011).
- Add Hirata et al. (2013) and Geng (2018) for cross-country macro context.

### If your main question is "How should I build house-price or rent research datasets?"
- Use Bailey, Muth, and Nourse (1963) and Case and Shiller (1987) for repeat-sales logic.
- Use Hill (2011) for hedonic indices and mixed-quality stock.
- Use Rosen (1974) as the conceptual basis for feature engineering.

## 11. Environmental Disamenities, Flood Risk, And Climate-Risk Capitalization

### Kenneth Y. Chay and Michael Greenstone (2005), *Does Air Quality Matter? Evidence from the Housing Market*
- Type: Strong causal environmental-capitalization paper; Journal of Political Economy.
- Summary: A landmark quasi-experimental study showing that cleaner air raises housing values. It is one of the best causal papers on environmental amenities being capitalized into prices.
- Best use when: You want a rigorous reason to include air quality, pollution exposure, or environmental quality measures in housing-price models.
- Main factors: Air pollution, environmental quality, willingness to pay, capitalization.
- Why it matters for your project: This is a strong foundation for the broader environmental-disamenity literature, not just flood or climate risk.
- Links: [NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w6826), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1086/427462)

### Janet Currie, Lucas Davis, Michael Greenstone, and Reed Walker (2015), *Environmental Health Risks and Housing Values: Evidence from 1,600 Toxic Plant Openings and Closings*
- Type: Top empirical environmental paper; American Economic Review.
- Summary: Uses plant openings and closings to identify the price effect of local toxic exposure. It shows that housing values respond sharply to nearby environmental health risk.
- Best use when: You want evidence on localized environmental disamenities such as industrial hazards, emissions, or contamination.
- Main factors: Toxic exposure, environmental health risk, local externalities, property values.
- Why it matters for your project: Particularly useful if you later add industrial-site, contamination, or pollution-adjacency features.
- Links: [AEA/PubMed details](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27134284/), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1257/aer.20121656)

### Okmyung Bin, Jamie Brown Kruse, and Craig E. Landry (2008), *Flood Hazards, Insurance Rates, and Amenities: Evidence from the Coastal Housing Market*
- Type: Foundational flood-risk capitalization paper; Journal of Risk and Insurance.
- Summary: A core paper showing that flood-zone designation and insurance premiums are capitalized into coastal property values, while coastal amenities can offset some risk penalties.
- Best use when: You need a baseline paper on how flood risk enters prices through both hazard and insurance channels.
- Main factors: Flood zones, insurance premiums, amenities, coastal risk.
- Why it matters for your project: Excellent starting point if you plan to merge flood maps, insurance proxies, or coastline exposure into pricing work.
- Links: [IDEAS/RePEc](https://ideas.repec.org/a/bla/jrinsu/v75y2008i1p63-82.html), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1539-6975.2007.00248.x)

### Allan Beltran, David Maddison, and Robert Elliott (2019), *The Impact of Flooding on Property Prices: A Repeat-Sales Approach*
- Type: Strong UK flood-risk paper; Journal of Environmental Economics and Management.
- Summary: Uses repeat-sales data from England to estimate the post-flood price path of affected properties. It finds sharp post-flood discounts that fade over time but persist longer for lower-priced homes.
- Best use when: You want UK-specific evidence on realized flood events rather than only ex ante flood-zone exposure.
- Main factors: Flood events, repeat sales, price recovery, heterogeneous impacts.
- Why it matters for your project: One of the most directly relevant papers if your real-estate work is England-focused.
- Links: [University of Birmingham page](https://research.birmingham.ac.uk/en/publications/the-impact-of-flooding-on-property-prices-a-repeat-sales-approach), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jeem.2019.02.006)

### Asaf Bernstein, Matthew T. Gustafson, and Ryan Lewis (2019), *Disaster on the Horizon: The Price Effect of Sea Level Rise*
- Type: Highly cited climate-risk pricing paper; Journal of Financial Economics.
- Summary: Shows that homes exposed to long-run sea-level-rise risk trade at a discount, even when the physical hazard is far in the future.
- Best use when: You want evidence that markets can price long-horizon climate risk before near-term damage is realized.
- Main factors: Sea level rise, long-run climate risk, coastal exposure, risk capitalization.
- Why it matters for your project: A key paper for the climate-risk side of real-estate pricing.
- Links: [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X19300807), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2019.03.013)

### Markus Baldauf, Lorenzo Garlappi, and Constantine Yannelis (2020), *Does Climate Change Affect Real Estate Prices? Only If You Believe In It*
- Type: Major climate-beliefs paper; Review of Financial Studies.
- Summary: Shows that climate-risk pricing depends partly on local beliefs. Similar physical exposures can be priced differently depending on whether local buyers take climate risk seriously.
- Best use when: You want to combine physical risk with information, beliefs, politics, or heterogeneous awareness.
- Main factors: Climate beliefs, information, sea-level exposure, heterogeneity in pricing.
- Why it matters for your project: Very useful when thinking about why pricing of physical risk may be incomplete, delayed, or spatially uneven.
- Links: [Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/rfs/article/33/3/1256/5735306), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1093/rfs/hhz073)

### Benjamin J. Keys and Philip Mulder (2020), *Neglected No More: Housing Markets, Mortgage Lending, and Sea Level Rise*
- Type: Strong housing-finance climate paper; NBER Working Paper.
- Summary: Shows that transaction volumes in highly exposed Florida markets weakened before sale prices clearly adjusted, which is a valuable result for understanding market liquidity under emerging climate risk.
- Best use when: You want to study how climate risk first shows up in liquidity, turnover, and market participation, not just price levels.
- Main factors: Sea level rise, transaction volume, mortgage markets, delayed price adjustment.
- Why it matters for your project: Especially useful if you later analyze listing activity, turnover, or liquidity alongside prices.
- Links: [NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w27930), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.3386/w27930)

### How to use this branch
- Start with Chay and Greenstone (2005) if you want the broad environmental-capitalization logic.
- Add Currie et al. (2015) for highly local environmental disamenities.
- Add Bin, Kruse, and Landry (2008) plus Beltran, Maddison, and Elliott (2019) for flood-risk and flood-event work.
- Add Bernstein, Gustafson, and Lewis (2019), Baldauf, Garlappi, and Yannelis (2020), and Keys and Mulder (2020) for long-horizon climate risk and market awareness.
- Watch-out: Physical risk can affect volume, liquidity, insurance, and financing before it shows up fully in transaction prices.

## 12. Listing-Market Dynamics, Repeat Listings, Time On Market, And Price Reductions

### John R. Knight (2002), *Listing Price, Time on Market, and Ultimate Selling Price: Causes and Effects of Listing Price Changes*
- Type: Core listing-dynamics paper; Real Estate Economics.
- Summary: A foundational paper on listing-price revisions. It studies why sellers cut prices and how those revisions relate to time on market and final sale outcomes.
- Best use when: You want one of the clearest starting points for research based on asking prices rather than only closed-sale prices.
- Main factors: Listing-price changes, time on market, seller behavior, final sale price.
- Why it matters for your project: Directly relevant if your raw data include listings, relistings, price cuts, or stale inventory.
- Links: [EconBiz entry](https://www.econbiz.de/Record/listing-price-time-on-market-and-ultimate-selling-price-causes-and-effects-of-listing-price-changes-knight-john/10006895398), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/1540-6229.00038)

### Antonio Merlo and François Ortalo-Magné (2004), *Bargaining over Residential Real Estate: Evidence from England*
- Type: Major microstructure paper with UK data; Journal of Urban Economics.
- Summary: A classic England-based paper using rich transaction histories with listing-price changes and offers. It documents stylized facts on bargaining, timing, and sale agreement formation.
- Best use when: You want UK evidence on offers, list-price revisions, and the sequence from listing to sale.
- Main factors: Bargaining, offers, listing revisions, sale timing.
- Why it matters for your project: One of the best papers if you want to model the listing process itself rather than just the final sale.
- Links: [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0094119004000609), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jue.2004.05.004)

### David Genesove and Lu Han (2012), *Search and Matching in the Housing Market*
- Type: Important housing-liquidity paper; Journal of Urban Economics.
- Summary: Studies how demand affects time on market, buyer search, and seller matching outcomes. It helps explain housing-market liquidity, not just prices.
- Best use when: You want a stronger theoretical and empirical base for time on market, market tightness, and search frictions.
- Main factors: Search frictions, liquidity, time on market, buyer-seller matching.
- Why it matters for your project: Useful if you plan to analyze listings as a market-clearing process rather than a static price panel.
- Links: [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0094119012000034), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jue.2012.01.002)

### Lu Han and William C. Strange (2016), *What Is the Role of the Asking Price for a House?*
- Type: Strong asking-price paper; Journal of Urban Economics.
- Summary: Shows that asking prices matter because they direct buyer search, even though homes do not always sell exactly at the posted price.
- Best use when: You want a theory-backed reason to treat the asking price as an economically meaningful variable instead of just marketing noise.
- Main factors: Asking price, buyer search, market tightness, atypical homes.
- Why it matters for your project: Very helpful when deciding whether to model asking price, sale-to-list ratio, and search activity jointly.
- Links: [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0094119016300018), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jue.2016.03.008)

### Cross-reference: David Genesove and Christopher Mayer (2001), *Loss Aversion and Seller Behavior: Evidence from the Housing Market*
- Type: Behavioral pricing paper; already listed in Section 3.
- Summary: Explains why sellers resist nominal losses, which contributes to sticky list prices and slow downward adjustment.
- Best use when: You want a behavioral interpretation of price reductions, stale listings, or reluctance to reprice.
- Main factors: Loss aversion, list-price stickiness, seller reference points.
- Why it matters for your project: This is one of the best complements to the listing-process papers above.

### How to use this branch
- Start with Knight (2002) for direct evidence on list-price revisions.
- Add Merlo and Ortalo-Magné (2004) if you want UK evidence with offer histories.
- Add Genesove and Han (2012) for search frictions and time-on-market mechanics.
- Add Han and Strange (2016) when you want to justify using asking price as a meaningful explanatory variable.
- Watch-out: Portal or broker listing data often miss withdrawn listings, relistings under new identifiers, and private offer histories, so measured time on market can be biased downward.

## 13. Rental-Side Extensions: Landlord Behavior, Vacancy, And Housing Stock Filtering

### John F. Sweeney (1974), *A Commodity Hierarchy Model of the Rental Housing Market*
- Type: Foundational filtering theory; Journal of Urban Economics.
- Summary: A classic model of the rental market as a quality hierarchy in which housing ages, deteriorates, and filters down through the quality distribution.
- Best use when: You want the theoretical base for housing-stock filtering and quality downgrading over time.
- Main factors: Filtering, quality hierarchy, deterioration, rental stock dynamics.
- Why it matters for your project: This is the key early theory paper for thinking about how older housing becomes lower-rent housing.
- Links: [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/0094119074900102), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/0094-1190(74)90010-2)

### William C. Wheaton (1990), *Vacancy, Search, and Prices in a Housing Market Matching Model*
- Type: Core vacancy and liquidity theory paper; Journal of Political Economy.
- Summary: Develops a housing-market matching model linking vacancy, search duration, and prices. It is central for understanding structural vacancy and market liquidity.
- Best use when: You want a theory of how vacancy rates, time to let or sell, and prices interact.
- Main factors: Vacancy, search, matching, reservation prices, liquidity.
- Why it matters for your project: Useful if your rental work later expands beyond rent levels into vacancy and market tightness.
- Links: [IDEAS/RePEc](https://ideas.repec.org/a/ucp/jpolec/v98y1990i6p1270-92.html), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1086/261734)

### Edward L. Glaeser and Joseph Gyourko (2005), *Urban Decline and Durable Housing*
- Type: Important durable-stock paper; Journal of Political Economy / NBER.
- Summary: Shows how durable housing stock shapes urban decline, low prices, and persistent occupancy patterns when demand weakens.
- Best use when: You want to understand why housing supply does not disappear quickly and why weak-demand places can have low prices and persistent vacancies.
- Main factors: Durable stock, urban decline, vacancies, price asymmetry.
- Why it matters for your project: Particularly relevant if you study regional divergence, older stock, or low-demand submarkets.
- Links: [NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w8598), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.3386/w8598)

### Stuart S. Rosenthal (2014), *Are Private Markets and Filtering a Viable Source of Low-Income Housing? Estimates from a "Repeat Income" Model*
- Type: Major empirical filtering paper; American Economic Review.
- Summary: Provides direct estimates of filtering rates and shows that private markets do generate lower-income housing over time, especially in the rental stock.
- Best use when: You want the strongest empirical paper on filtering as a mechanism producing affordable housing.
- Main factors: Filtering, affordability, tenure transitions, housing age.
- Why it matters for your project: One of the most important papers if you later study affordability, stock aging, or low-rent supply.
- Links: [AEA](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Faer.104.2.687), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1257/aer.104.2.687)

### David P. Sims (2007), *Out of Control: What Can We Learn from the End of Massachusetts Rent Control?*
- Type: Strong rental-policy paper; Journal of Urban Economics.
- Summary: Examines how ending rent control affected rents, quality, and tenure status. It is especially useful for landlord-side responses and maintenance incentives.
- Best use when: You want evidence on how regulation changes landlord behavior, unit quality, and the size of the rental stock.
- Main factors: Rent control, landlord response, rental quality, tenure conversion.
- Why it matters for your project: A strong complement to Diamond, McQuade, and Qian (2019) if you want more evidence on landlord-side adjustment.
- Links: [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0094119006000635), [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jue.2006.06.004)

### Cross-reference: Rebecca Diamond, Tim McQuade, and Franklin Qian (2019), *The Effects of Rent Control Expansion on Tenants, Landlords, and Inequality*
- Type: Rental regulation and landlord-response paper; already listed in Section 7.
- Summary: Particularly useful for long-run landlord and supply responses to rental regulation.
- Best use when: You want a high-profile modern paper on policy-induced landlord behavior.
- Main factors: Rent control, landlord response, supply reduction, tenant composition.
- Why it matters for your project: This is the most direct bridge between rental regulation and landlord behavior in the existing library.

### How to use this branch
- Start with Sweeney (1974) for the theory of filtering.
- Add Rosenthal (2014) for the best direct empirical evidence on filtering rates.
- Add Wheaton (1990) when vacancy and market tightness matter.
- Add Glaeser and Gyourko (2005) for durable-stock dynamics in weak-demand places.
- Add Sims (2007) and Diamond, McQuade, and Qian (2019) for landlord behavior under regulation.
- Watch-out: Rental-market outcomes can move through several margins at once: rents, vacancy, maintenance quality, tenure conversion, and long-run stock composition.
